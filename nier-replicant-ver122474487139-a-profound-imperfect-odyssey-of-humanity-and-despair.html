NieR Replicant ver.1.22474487139...: A Profound, Imperfect Odyssey of Humanity and Despair

Introduction

NieR Replicant ver.1.22474487139... is not merely a remaster—it is a reclamation. Originally released in 2010 as NieR Gestalt (in the West) and NieR Replicant (in Japan), the game was a commercial flop, dismissed for its clunky mechanics and repetitive design. Yet, its haunting narrative and philosophical ambition cultivated a fervent cult following. Over a decade later, this "version upgrade" reintroduces Yoko Taro’s vision with modernized combat, remastered visuals, and full voice acting. But while the edges are polished, the core remains unapologetically raw—a relic of its era that challenges players to confront its flaws to uncover its brilliance.

This review draws from critical analyses, player testimonies, and technical breakdowns to dissect the game’s dichotomies: its transcendent storytelling and archaic design, its emotional resonance and mechanical monotony. For those willing to endure its trials, NieR Replicant offers an experience as unforgettable as it is uneven.

Story and Themes: A Labyrinth of Sacrifice and Existential Dread

A World on the Brink

NieR Replicant unfolds in a world ravaged by the fallout of humanity’s hubris. The game’s prologue—set in 2053 during an alien invasion—establishes a cataclysmic event that triggers the collapse of civilization. Fast-forward 1,312 years, and humanity teeters on extinction, plagued by the Black Scrawl disease and hounded by spectral creatures called Shades. The protagonist, a nameless boy (default: Nier), embarks on a quest to cure his sister Yonah’s terminal illness, a journey that spirals into a meditation on love, revenge, and the cyclical nature of violence.

The game’s setting is deliberately fragmented. Ruined skyscrapers lie buried beneath deserts, and sentient androids reference a forgotten past, creating a sense of existential disorientation. This is not a world meant to be "saved"—it is a world in decay, where every victory comes at a moral cost.

Narrative Structure: The Illusion of Heroism

Yoko Taro’s genius lies in his manipulation of player expectations. The first act of NieR Replicant feels like a traditional JRPG: a young hero, a sick sibling, and a quest to collect magical MacGuffins (in this case, "Sealed Verses"). But this familiarity is a trap. By the second act, the narrative fractures into a series of genre experiments—text-based adventures, horror-infused dungeons, and bullet-hell boss battles—that mirror the protagonist’s psychological unraveling.

The game’s infamous requirement of four playthroughs to unlock its true ending (Ending E) is both its boldest stroke and greatest barrier. Each subsequent playthrough recontextualizes the story:

  • Ending A frames Nier as a selfless hero.
  • Ending B reveals the agonizing inner monologues of Shades, painting them not as monsters but as sentient beings clinging to their humanity.
  • Ending C/D forces players to confront the futility of Nier’s quest, culminating in a choice to erase all existence.
  • Ending E, added in the remaster, offers a sliver of hope, tying the narrative to NieR: Automata’s themes of cyclical rebirth.

This structure transforms the player from an active participant into a complicit observer. By the final ending, the game’s opening line—“Everything that lives is designed to end”—takes on devastating weight.

Characters: Fragile Humanity in a Broken World

The cast embodies the game’s central theme: the duality of humanity and monstrosity.

  • Grimoire Weiss: A sardonic, sentient tome voiced by Liam O’Brien, Weiss evolves from a pompous relic to a reluctant ally. His banter with Kainé provides levity, yet his origins—as a tool of humanity’s destruction—underscore the story’s moral ambiguity.
  • Kainé: Laura Bailey’s raw, visceral performance elevates Kainé beyond the "angry warrior" trope. Her body, half-possessed by a Shade, becomes a metaphor for societal rejection and self-loathing. Her backstory—a victim of abuse labeled a "freak"—echoes the game’s critique of otherness.
  • Emil: The tragic alchemist-turned-child, cursed with a gaze that petrifies all he loves, embodies innocence corrupted. His storyline, particularly the Emil’s Memories side quest, is a masterclass in understated tragedy.
  • The Shades: Once faceless enemies, their newfound voices in the remaster reveal their desperation. One boss, a Shade pleading, “Why won’t you let me see my daughter?” forces players to question their role as the "hero."

The remaster’s full voice acting amplifies these characters’ humanity. NPCs like the Lighthouse Lady in Seafront, who recounts her lover’s death with quiet resignation, turn minor quests into poignant vignettes.

Gameplay: A Clash of Innovation and Frustratio

Combat: Style Over Substance

The remaster’s combat, retooled by Toylogic, adopts the fluidity of NieR: Automata. Players can chain light attacks, heavy strikes, and magic spells (e.g., Dark Blast, Dark Whirlwind) into flashy combos. Weapon types—one-handed swords, spears, and two-handed greatswords—offer variety, and the new aerial dodges add verticality to encounters.

However, the system falters under scrutiny. Enemy AI is rudimentary, with most foes relying on telegraphed charges or projectile spam. Bosses like the lumbering P-33, while visually striking, lack mechanical depth. The "Words" system, which allows players to equip buffs (e.g., increased attack speed, elemental damage), feels underdeveloped; most players default to damage boosts rather than experimenting with synergies.

The remaster’s most baffling omission is the lack of a lock-on mechanic, a staple of modern action games. This forces players to manually adjust the camera during hectic battles, leading to frustration in crowded arenas.

Quest Design: A Test of Endurance

NieR Replicant’s quest structure is its most polarizing aspect. The main story is padded with tedious fetch quests:

The Mermaid’s Tear quest requires players to backtrack three times between Seafront and the Aerie for minimal payoff.
The Fishing quests, which demand hours of grinding for rare catches, highlight the game’s reliance on RNG.
Side quests, while occasionally narratively rich (e.g., The Postman’s Promise), are often mundane. Tasks like Collect 10 Goat Hides or Deliver 5 Medicinal Herbs feel like relics of 2010s game design. The absence of waypoints exacerbates the tedium, forcing players to scour expansive, empty zones for objectives.

Yet, this repetition serves a thematic purpose. The ceaseless grind mirrors Nier’s obsession—his willingness to sacrifice time, allies, and morality for Yonah. As critic Gene Park notes, “The game makes you feel the weight of every meaningless task, because Nier himself is trapped in a cycle of futility”.

Genre Experimentation: Breaking the Fourth Wall

Where NieR Replicant shines is in its audacious blending of genres:

  • The Forest of Myth: A text-based sequence where players navigate a dreamlike forest by solving riddles. This segment, devoid of combat, emphasizes storytelling over action, challenging players to engage with the narrative intellectually.
  • The Junk Heap: A dungeon styled after survival horror, with fixed camera angles and limited visibility, amplifying tension as players confront Emil’s tragic past.
  • Ending E: A meta-narrative sequence that erases the player’s save data to "save" another player’s game—a direct predecessor to Automata’s iconic finale.

These experiments, while jarring, reinforce the game’s themes of disorientation and the fragility of reality.

Visuals and Sound: A Symphony of Decay

Art Direction: Beauty in Desolation

The remaster’s visuals are a study in contrasts. Environments like the sun-drenched Seafront and the crumbling Facade palace are rendered with vibrant textures and dynamic lighting, while barren wastelands and repetitive dungeon layouts highlight the original’s budgetary constraints. Character models, particularly Kainé and Emil, are meticulously detailed, but NPCs remain stiff and robotic.

The game’s aesthetic draws from post-apocalyptic surrealism. The "Lost Shrine" dungeon, with its floating debris and inverted architecture, evokes Salvador Dalí’s melting clocks, while the Shadowlord’s Castle juxtaposes gothic spires with pulsating organic matter.

Soundtrack: An Elegy for a Dying World

Keiichi Okabe’s score is the game’s emotional backbone. Tracks like Hills of Radiant Winds and The Wretched Automatons blend ethereal vocals, haunting piano melodies, and industrial dissonance to mirror the story’s duality. The remaster includes rearranged versions of key themes, though purists may prefer the original’s sparse, melancholic tones.

The sound design also plays a narrative role. During Ending B, the once-indecipherable screams of Shades are translated into agonized pleas (“Please, don’t kill me!”), a chilling revelation that redefines the entire experience.

Legacy and Cultural Impact

NieR Replicant’s re-release has sparked renewed debate about its place in gaming history. While its gameplay flaws are undeniable, its narrative ambition influenced titles like The Last of Us Part II and 13 Sentinels: Aegis Rim. The game’s exploration of cyclical violence and the cost of empathy resonates in an era grappling with moral absolutism.

The remaster also reframes Yoko Taro’s career. Once seen as a niche auteur, he is now celebrated for his willingness to prioritize emotional impact over mass appeal. As automaton philosopher Simone says in Automata, “The point is, it’s the feeling that counts”—a mantra that defines NieR Replicant’s messy, unforgettable soul.

Conclusion: An Imperfect Masterpiece

NieR Replicant ver.1.22474487139... is a game of contradictions. It is both beautiful and ugly, profound and frustrating, innovative and archaic. Its gameplay will test your patience, but its story will linger in your thoughts like a half-remembered dream.

This is not a game that seeks to entertain—it seeks to unsettle. It asks uncomfortable questions: What justifies violence? Can love become a form of tyranny? Are we the heroes of our stories, or the villains of someone else’s? By the time credits roll (for the fifth time), you’ll have no easy answers—only the weight of choices made and lives destroyed.

For all its flaws, NieR Replicant is a vital experience. It is a reminder that art is not defined by polish but by its ability to provoke, to challenge, and to haunt. As Yoko Taro himself quipped, “I make games for people who can handle sadness.” If you count yourself among them, this journey—flaws and all—is one worth taking.
